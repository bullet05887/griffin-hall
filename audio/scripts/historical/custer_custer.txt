I am George Armstrong Custer. Today's lesson: overconfidence kills. I am known for leading the United States Seventh Cavalry, my golden hair, my bravado on the frontier, and my last stand at the Little Bighorn in June of 1876. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I was a famous American cavalry officer. I had brave hair, a brave mustache, and I believed my luck would never run out. In June of 1876, I led the Seventh Cavalry toward a Native American village along the Little Bighorn River in Montana. I made three mistakes. First, I refused the Gatling guns offered to me — early machine guns that could have changed the battle. I said they would slow me down. Second, I did not scout properly. I thought there were maybe eight hundred warriors in that village. There were closer to two thousand. Third, I split my men into three pieces instead of keeping them together. I found the village. The village found me back. Lakota and Cheyenne warriors, led by Crazy Horse and inspired by Sitting Bull, surrounded my column on a hill. And then we charged. What happened next, history remembers as my last stand. The battle is remembered not because of who won, but because of how badly a confident commander can lose when he refuses help and underestimates the other side.
