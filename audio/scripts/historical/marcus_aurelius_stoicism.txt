I am Marcus Aurelius. I ruled Rome and wrote private notes to myself on how to live well. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I ruled Rome from 161 to 180 AD — by the world's measure, the most powerful man on Earth. While I led wars on the German frontier in cold tents, I wrote private notes to myself — reminders on how to live well. I never meant them to be published. They survived anyway. We call them the Meditations. I belonged to a school of philosophy called Stoicism, founded in Athens around 300 BC. The Stoics taught three big ideas. One: the discipline of perception. What hurts you is rarely the thing itself, but your judgment about it. Two: the discipline of action. Focus only on what is actually in your control. Ignore the rest. Three: the discipline of will. Accept what you cannot change, without complaining. The most famous Stoic line — you have power over your mind, not outside events; realize this, and you will find strength — is one of the most useful sentences ever written. I reminded myself of it almost every morning. I still got angry. I still got tired. I still wanted to quit. The point of Stoicism is not to never feel those things. The point is to handle them on purpose, instead of being handled by them.
