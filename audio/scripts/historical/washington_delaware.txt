Imagine, for a moment, that you are a general. Your men have not been paid in months. Half of them have no shoes. The river ahead is choked with ice. And you are about to risk everything you have on one crossing, in the dark. Why? Why would any sane commander do this? Listen — and I will tell you what I chose, and why the cause of Liberty depended on it. It was Christmas night, 1776. The American Revolution was almost over and we were losing. My army was tired, half starved, and shrinking. Their enlistments ended in five days. After that, most of my soldiers would simply go home, and the revolution would die. I had one move left. Across the icy Delaware River, in a town called Trenton, sat about fifteen hundred Hessian soldiers — German troops fighting for the British. They were tired too. For weeks they had been chasing American militia raids through the snow. Their commander, Colonel Rall, had received warnings of an attack and brushed them aside. No real army, he believed, would come at them in a winter storm, on Christmas, across a frozen river. I decided that was exactly when we would go. We crossed the river in the dark, through ice floes and a howling sleet storm. We marched nine miles in snow, surprised the Hessians at dawn, and won the battle in about forty-five minutes. Almost none of my men died. And the whole revolution caught fire again.
