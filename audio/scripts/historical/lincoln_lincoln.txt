I am Abraham Lincoln. Today's lesson: leadership in crisis. I am known for serving as the sixteenth President of the United States, holding the Union together through four years of civil war, and ending slavery in America. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I was born in 1809. Within weeks of my inauguration in 1861, eleven southern states had seceded over slavery, and the Civil War began. For four years, more than six hundred thousand Americans died — more than in every other American war combined. I carried that weight alone. I was ruthless when ruthlessness was required. I suspended habeas corpus, jailing thousands without trial. I approved General William Tecumseh Sherman's march through Georgia in 1864, burning farms, factories, and railroads from Atlanta to the sea to break the South's ability to fight. I fired generals who would not attack, until I found Ulysses S. Grant, who would. I did what was needed to win the war. And I was merciful when mercy was required. In my second inaugural address, six weeks before the end, with victory in sight, I said: with malice toward none, with charity for all, let us strive on to finish the work we are in, to bind up the nation's wounds. Five days after Confederate General Lee surrendered at Appomattox, I went to Ford's Theater on the night of the fourteenth of April, 1865. That night was my last. The Union held — because I had carried both the sword and the open hand at the same time.
