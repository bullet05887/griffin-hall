I am Winston Churchill. Today's lesson: standing alone. I am known for standing as British Prime Minister in the dark year of 1940 when Britain stood alone against Hitler, and refusing — in speeches the whole free world heard — to ever surrender. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. In May of 1940, France was collapsing in days against the Nazi blitzkrieg. The British Expeditionary Force was trapped on the beaches of Dunkirk. Many in our government, including the Foreign Secretary, Lord Halifax, wanted to negotiate peace with Hitler through Mussolini. It looked like the realistic choice. We were alone in Europe. America had not yet entered the war. The Soviets were on Hitler's side at that point. Hitler controlled the continent. I became Prime Minister on the tenth of May, 1940. In a closed cabinet meeting on the twenty-eighth of May, I listened to the arguments for negotiating. Then I refused. I told my ministers: if this long island story of ours is to end at last, let it end only when each one of us lies choking in his own blood upon the ground. That was the moment. Britain would fight. I went to Parliament and delivered the speeches that became some of the most famous words in the English language. We shall fight on the beaches. We shall fight on the landing grounds. We shall never surrender. Britain held alone for a year. The Battle of Britain, summer of 1940, was won by the Royal Air Force. Hitler's planned invasion of these islands was canceled. America entered the war in December of 1941. Hitler eventually lost. None of that happens if I fold in May of 1940.
