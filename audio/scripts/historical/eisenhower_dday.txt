I am Dwight D. Eisenhower. I commanded the largest sea invasion in history on the beaches of Normandy. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. By 1944, Nazi Germany had taken over most of Europe. The free countries — America, Britain, Canada, and many others — had to invade France to push the Nazis back. The Germans had built giant concrete bunkers, machine gun nests, and beach obstacles all along the French coast. They expected an invasion. They did not know when, or where. As the Allied commander, I picked a fifty-mile stretch of beach in Normandy, France. On the sixth of June, 1944, before sunrise, I sent paratroopers behind German lines to cut off reinforcements. Then thousands of bombers hit the bunkers. Then warships blasted the beaches from the sea. Then — and only then — I landed one hundred and fifty-six thousand soldiers on those beaches. It was the largest sea invasion in history. It worked because every piece — air, sea, paratroopers, infantry, even fake radio messages telling the Germans the real attack was somewhere else — moved together at the right time. We call this combined arms. One arm alone fails. All of them together, in sync, opened a hole in Hitler's wall.
