I am Augustus Caesar. Today's lesson: builder of empire. I am known for becoming the first emperor of Rome at the age of nineteen, ending a century of civil wars, and founding the Pax Romana — two centuries of Roman peace. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. When my great-uncle and adopted father, Julius Caesar, was murdered in 44 BC, I was an eighteen-year-old named Gaius Octavius. Nobody took me seriously. I was sickly, young, and not a soldier. Over the next seventeen years, I outmaneuvered every rival, including the famous general Mark Antony and Queen Cleopatra of Egypt, and emerged as the sole ruler of Rome in 27 BC. The Senate gave me a new name — Augustus, the revered one. Where Caesar had been loud, proud, and openly ambitious, I was quiet and patient. I never called myself king or emperor. I pretended to restore the republic while quietly controlling every important position myself. I let other people take credit. I moved slowly. I outlasted my enemies. Most of them died before they could act against me. I ruled for forty-one years, 27 BC to 14 AD, and I built the Pax Romana — the Roman peace — roughly two centuries of relative peace and prosperity across the Mediterranean. I standardized money, built roads, organized the army, created a postal system, and set up institutions strong enough to hold the empire together long after I was gone. Caesar gave Rome a sword. I gave it a structure. The structure lasted centuries. The sword did not.
