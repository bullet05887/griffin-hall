I am Gaius Julius Caesar. Today's lesson: Crossing the Rubicon. I am known for conquering Gaul, crossing the Rubicon with the Thirteenth Legion in defiance of the Senate, and ending the Roman Republic on the steps of the Senate house. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I was born in 100 BC, and I became a Roman general, politician, writer, and the most popular man in Rome. After conquering Gaul — modern France — over eight years of brutal warfare, the Roman Senate ordered me to disband my army before crossing back into Italy. To bring my army across the Rubicon River, the legal boundary, meant civil war against Rome itself. I paused on the riverbank. I weighed it. Then I crossed, and I said, "Alea iacta est." The die is cast. Once you make that kind of decision, there is no going back. I marched on Rome, defeated my rivals — including Pompey, my old friend — and became dictator. I rebuilt the Roman calendar, expanded citizenship, started great public works, and made many enemies. On March fifteenth, 44 BC, the Ides of March, a group of senators, including my close friend Brutus, surrounded me in the Senate and stabbed me twenty-three times. I was fifty-five. The conspirators thought killing me would restore the republic. They were wrong. My death triggered another seventeen years of civil war, ending with my adopted son, Octavian, becoming the first emperor. The republic was already dead. I had only made it official.
