I am Alexander of Macedon. By thirty I had conquered from Greece to India and lost no battle. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I inherited a small Greek kingdom at the age of twenty, when my father, Philip the Second, was assassinated. By thirty-two, I had conquered the entire Persian Empire — the largest empire the world had ever seen — from Greece across Egypt, all the way to the Indus River in modern Pakistan. I never lost a single major battle. My most famous moment: the Gordian Knot. An ancient prophecy said whoever untied a hopelessly tangled knot in the city of Gordium would rule all of Asia. Wise men had tried for centuries. I looked at it, drew my sword, and cut the knot in half. The lesson: when the old rules of a problem will not let you win, change the rules. My tragedy: I could not stop. My soldiers, after eight years of marching, finally refused to go any further at the Hyphasis River in India. I had to turn back. In 323 BC, in Babylon, I lay down with a sudden fever — exhausted from a decade of nonstop war. I was thirty-two. My empire fell apart within decades, because I had never built the institutions to hold it together. I had only conquered. Vision without continuation dies with the visionary.
