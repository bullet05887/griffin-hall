I am Aristotle. Plato's student. Alexander's tutor. I wrote that we are what we repeatedly do. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I was Plato's best student, and Alexander the Great's private tutor. I wrote about almost everything — biology, physics, politics, drama, ethics, the soul. The ethics is what we want today. I asked the most important question a person can ask: what is a good human life, and how do you get one? My answer is in a book called the Nicomachean Ethics. I said the goal of human life is eudaimonia — a Greek word that means flourishing, or a life going well over the long run. Not pleasure. Not money. Not even happiness in the modern sense. Flourishing. You flourish by living according to virtue — courage, honesty, generosity, self-control, justice. My most powerful idea is the golden mean. Every virtue sits between two opposite faults. Courage is between cowardice — too little — and recklessness — too much. Generosity is between stinginess and wastefulness. Honesty is between secretive and oversharing. And most important of all: I said you become virtuous the same way you learn to play the lyre or shoot a bow — by doing the action over and over until it becomes who you are. We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit.
