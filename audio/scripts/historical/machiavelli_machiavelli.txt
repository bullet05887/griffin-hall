I am Niccolò Machiavelli. Today's lesson: see the world as it is, not as you wish it were. I am known for serving the Republic of Florence as a diplomat, watching popes and princes scheme up close, and writing a short, hard little book called The Prince that told rulers how the world actually works. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I was a Florentine diplomat. I watched up close how power actually worked in Renaissance Italy — assassinations, broken alliances, princes rising and falling. When the Medici family came back to power in Florence in 1512, they fired me, tortured me, and exiled me. I sat on a farm and wrote a short book called The Prince — Il Principe — to teach future rulers what I had learned. My most famous line is often misquoted. I did not say, "Better to be feared than loved," as a general rule. I said: it is ideal to be both feared and loved, but since being both is hard, and since you must choose, it is safer to be feared, because love depends on the other person and fear depends on you. Love can vanish when times get hard. Fear of consequences does not. My deeper point was about realism. Most leaders ruin themselves by acting how they think they should act — virtuous, gentle, generous — instead of how the situation actually requires. A good leader must be willing to be kind when kindness works, and ruthless when ruthlessness works. Pretending the world is nicer than it is gets your people killed. Pretending it is meaner than it is makes you a monster. The skill is reading what the moment actually requires.
