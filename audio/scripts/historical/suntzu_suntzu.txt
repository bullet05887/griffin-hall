I am Sun Tzu. Today's lesson: The Art of War. I am known for writing thirteen short chapters called The Art of War, twenty-five centuries ago in ancient China, that are still read by generals, athletes, and merchants today. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. About two thousand five hundred years ago, in ancient China, I wrote a short book. Thirteen short chapters. Generals, athletes, business owners, and even chess players still read it. Why? Because most of it is not really about war at all. It is about thinking clearly when other people are panicking. My biggest idea is this: the greatest victory is the battle not fought. If you can win without fighting — by being so well prepared the other side gives up, or by getting what you wanted through trades and talking — that is the best win. Real warriors do not love fighting. They love winning, which is different. I also taught that you must know yourself and your opponent. Knowing only yourself, you will win half. Knowing only your opponent, you will win half. Know both, and you can fight a hundred battles without disaster. Know neither, and every fight is a coin flip.
