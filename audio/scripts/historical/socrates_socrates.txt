I am Socrates. I asked simple questions until men admitted what they did not actually know. Listen, and I will tell you how it was. I lived in Athens. I never wrote a book. I walked the marketplace, asking people — politicians, generals, poets, craftsmen — what they really meant when they used big words like justice, courage, piety, goodness. Almost nobody could answer cleanly. The questions exposed how shaky most beliefs really are when you push on them. My method, now called the Socratic method, was simple. Ask a question. Listen. Ask a better question based on the answer. Repeat. I believed wisdom started with admitting what you do not know. My most famous line is, "I know that I know nothing" — which sounds humble but is actually a power move. I had figured out that most of the confident people around me knew less than they thought. Athens convicted me of corrupting the youth and of impiety — not respecting the city's gods — and sentenced me to death. I had a chance to escape. I refused. They handed me the cup of hemlock. I drank it. The rest is for others to tell. But know this: I lived by the principle I had taught my whole life, even when it cost me everything.
