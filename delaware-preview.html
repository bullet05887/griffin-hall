Washington Crossing the Delaware

Christmas night, 1776. A river of ice. A republic in the balance.

It was Christmas night, and the river was full of ice. The thermometer read twenty-nine degrees and falling. On the New Jersey side of the Delaware, fifteen hundred Hessian soldiers slept in the village of Trenton — professionals, paid mercenaries, the best infantry in Europe. They had every reason to believe the war was already over.

And it nearly was. General George Washington's army had lost New York. It had been chased across New Jersey by the British. It had shrunk from twenty thousand men to barely twenty-four hundred. The enlistments of most of those men would expire on the last day of December — six days from now. After that, Washington would have almost no army at all.

So Washington did something that no professional general would have done. He decided to attack — in a snowstorm, across an ice-clogged river, at three in the morning, on the night the Hessians would be most certain that no attack was coming.

The Crossing

The boats were Durham boats — flat-bottomed ore boats, forty feet long, built to carry iron down the Delaware. Colonel John Glover's Marblehead fishermen worked the oars in the dark, pushing chunks of ice aside with poles. The wind drove sleet horizontally into the men's faces. Two soldiers froze to death on the march from the landing.

Washington stood in the lead boat. He had told his men one thing before they pushed off: Victory or death. Those were the actual words on the password his officers used that night. Not a slogan. A statement of fact. If the attack failed, the army was finished. The Revolution was finished.

"I am determined, as the weather is so severe, to push on," Washington wrote that evening. "But the necessity, dire necessity, will — nay must — justify any attempt."

What the Hessians Did Not Know

The Hessian commander, Colonel Johann Rall, was a brave man and an experienced soldier. He had been warned that an attack might come. He had received the warnings, read them, and dismissed them. Washington's army was broken. Washington's army was finished. Washington's army would not be so foolish as to attack in this weather. He was wrong about all three.

At eight in the morning on December 26th, the Americans appeared out of the snow at the edge of Trenton. The fight lasted less than an hour. Colonel Rall was shot and killed. Almost a thousand Hessians surrendered. The Americans lost two men — both of them frozen on the march.

Reflect

Washington had every reason to wait. A professional general would have waited. What did he see that the professionals did not? And what does it mean that the future of a country sometimes turns on a single man refusing to do the sensible thing?

Why It Matters

Trenton did not win the war. The war went on for seven more years. But Trenton stopped the war from being lost. The American army stayed together. Men re-enlisted. Money came in. Foreign generals began to take Washington seriously. France would eventually take him seriously enough to send a navy.

A boy growing up in America inherits the republic Washington bought that night with twenty-four hundred half-frozen men in Durham boats. Every freedom you have — to speak, to worship, to leave, to disagree with your own government in public — was, on Christmas night in 1776, one foolish-looking decision away from never existing.

That is the kind of thing a boy ought to know.
